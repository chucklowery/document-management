# Opinion Article OP-SM-003 — A Ranked List Is One Path, Not the Structure of Knowledge

**Status:** Interpretive  
**Primary counterpoint:** Ted Nelson  
**Associated principles:** SM-P-001, SM-P-002, SM-P-006, SM-P-007  
**Associated requirements:** REQ-IR-005, REQ-IR-007, REQ-IR-011, REQ-IR-012, REQ-IR-013  
**Associated decisions:** ADR-IR-001

## Position

Ranking helps a user direct limited attention, but a relevance score must not flatten authored connections, alternatives, disagreement, source identity, or historical paths into one supposedly natural order.

A conventional ranked list encourages two mistakes: treating prominence as authority and treating the returned document as an isolated object. Both conflict with the system's hypertext principles.

## Nelson Counterpoint

A hypertext-oriented view treats links, transclusion, source identity, parallel documents, and alternative paths as fundamental. The user should be able to see where information came from, what points to it, how it differs from another version, and which authored Trail or Relationship gave it context.

Ranking is therefore a presentation and navigation policy, not the ontology of the corpus. The same sources may support a chronological view, an evidence path, a contradiction view, an authority-focused view, a Trail, or a relevance-ranked list.

## Opinion

The system should use ranking where it reduces search cost, but always identify the Ranking Policy and provide access to plural views and bidirectional Relationships. Result snippets should retain exact source and Revision identity. Relevance feedback should improve task-specific retrieval without turning popularity into truth or erasing less common perspectives.

The test of retrieval is not whether one list looks convincing. It is whether users can find relevant material, inspect its sources and alternatives, and move through the knowledge graph without losing authored context.
