# Authority and History Principles

These principles are normative for all downstream design.

## Consensus Shall Not Erase Disagreement

Competing interpretations, dissent, contradictory findings, alternative plans, and unresolved disputes may coexist. Synthesis shall not silently remove or demote material perspectives. Authority, confidence, dissent, uncertainty, and unresolved dispute remain explicit.

## Authority and Epistemic Status Are Contextual

Authority shall be explicit by purpose, organization, jurisdiction, effective period, bounded context, and decision scope. Popularity, repetition, graph centrality, automation, or publication do not establish truth. Consequential claims shall distinguish observation, report, inference, hypothesis, finding, recommendation, decision, projection, generated output, verification result, intended outcome, and observed outcome where applicable.

## Provenance Includes Selection, Transformation, and Omission

Provenance shall identify material sources, contributors, transformations, selection rules, applicable versions, and material exclusions. A synthesis shall not claim completeness when relevant material was unavailable, restricted, excluded, or unresolved.

## Historical Integrity Is Governed, Not Absolute

Historical preservation does not require universal permanent visibility or retention. The system shall support governed withdrawal, redaction, access revocation, legal deletion, consent revocation, erasure, and retention expiry while preserving the minimum lawful and safe accountability record.

## Authorization Shall Not Create False Completeness

A partial graph, projection, or answer shall not be presented as complete when relevant restricted information exists. Where lawful and safe, distinguish no related information, restricted related information, and indeterminate completeness.
