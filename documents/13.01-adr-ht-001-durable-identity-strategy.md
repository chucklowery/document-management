# Architecture Decision: Durable Identity Strategy

**Identifier:** ADR-HT-001

**Status:** Accepted

**Date:** 2026-06-22

**Project:** Organizational Knowledge and Work System

## Context

The system requires durable identity across bounded contexts, repositories, storage technologies, synchronization boundaries, exports, migrations, and long-lived references.

Identity is required for:

- Resources;
- Resource Revisions;
- Relationships;
- Content Regions;
- Trails;
- Trail Steps;
- Immutable Records;
- Projection Definitions;
- Projection Instances;
- external identity mappings.

Repository paths, names, database keys, source-control commits, content hashes, and external-system identifiers are not sufficiently stable as the principal cross-context identity.

The strategy must support decentralized creation, deterministic reference envelopes, independent reconstruction, governed erasure, and future replacement of any specific implementation.

## Decision

### 1. Principal identifiers use UUID version 7

Every principal managed identity shall use a randomly generated UUID version 7 represented in canonical lowercase hyphenated form.

Examples:

```text
018f6f4f-7bd4-7cc2-9b6d-778b8cdb40a1
```

The timestamp component supports operational ordering and index locality. It does not define business time, effective time, recorded time, or causal order.

### 2. Identity is opaque

A principal identifier shall not encode:

- mutable names;
- repository paths;
- organization names;
- security classification;
- lifecycle state;
- authority;
- semantic version;
- owning user;
- business meaning.

Kind, owning context, authority, and classification are separate contract fields.

### 3. Identity is never reused

Once assigned, an identifier shall never be reassigned to unrelated content, state, or meaning.

Renaming, movement, repository migration, storage migration, ownership changes, and external-system changes do not change identity.

Merges, splits, replacements, and corrections create explicit Relationships or records rather than reusing identity.

### 4. Resource and Revision identity are distinct

A Resource has one stable Resource Identity across its lifetime.

Each immutable Resource Revision receives a distinct Revision Identity and references its Resource Identity.

A Revision Identity does not imply ordering. Revision sequence, parentage, effective time, and recorded time are explicit fields or Relationships.

### 5. Content digest supports integrity, not principal identity

Immutable Revisions, records, manifests, and exported envelopes shall include a cryptographic content digest where practical.

The digest:

- verifies content integrity;
- supports deduplication and reconstruction checks;
- does not replace Resource, Revision, Relationship, or Record Identity;
- may change when canonical serialization rules change, even when domain identity does not.

The digest algorithm and canonicalization version are explicit metadata.

### 6. Relationship identity is first-class

Every consequential managed Relationship receives its own Relationship Identity.

Changing Relationship meaning, endpoints, selection policy, authority basis, or material provenance creates a new Relationship or Relationship Revision according to the owning contract. It does not silently mutate historical meaning.

Derived reverse indexes and Backlinks reference the same Relationship Identity and do not create a second authoritative Relationship.

### 7. Content Region identity survives ordinary movement

A Content Region receives a stable Region Identity stored in or alongside canonical semantic source.

A revision-specific Region Occurrence records where that Region appears within an exact Document Revision.

Moving or reformatting a Region without replacing its conceptual content preserves Region Identity while creating a new Region Occurrence.

Splitting, merging, or conceptually replacing a Region creates new Region Identities and explicit derivation or supersession Relationships.

### 8. Trail and Trail Step identity

A Trail has stable Trail Identity and immutable Trail Revision Identities.

A Trail Step receives stable Step Identity when it represents the same conceptual step across Trail Revisions.

The following create a new Step Identity unless an explicit migration decision says otherwise:

- conceptual replacement;
- split into multiple independently meaningful steps;
- merge of multiple steps;
- change from one referenced concept to an unrelated concept.

Reordering, annotation changes, or selection-policy changes may preserve Step Identity while creating a new Trail Revision.

### 9. Immutable Record identity

Every consequential Immutable Record receives its own Record Identity.

Corrections, reversals, supersessions, and amendments create new records linked to the original. They do not reuse or overwrite Record Identity.

### 10. Projection identity

A Projection Definition has stable identity and immutable definition Revisions.

Each materialized Projection Instance receives an Instance Identity and records:

- exact Projection Definition Revision;
- source identities and applicable Revisions or temporal boundary;
- generation time;
- policy and parameter values;
- relevant tool version;
- reproducibility digest where practical.

Two equivalent Projection results may have different Instance Identities. Equivalence is established by recorded inputs and reproducibility metadata, not identifier equality.

### 11. Cross-context reference envelope

Cross-context references use a documented envelope rather than a type-encoded identifier.

Minimum fields:

```json
{
  "id": "018f6f4f-7bd4-7cc2-9b6d-778b8cdb40a1",
  "kind": "Resource",
  "owning_context": "KnowledgeAndProvenance"
}
```

Revision-specific references additionally include `revision_id` or another explicitly defined historical selector.

Consumers treat unknown fields as forward-compatible metadata unless policy requires rejection.

### 12. External identities remain mappings

External identifiers are stored through explicit External Identity Mapping records containing:

- local identity;
- external system identity;
- external identifier;
- authority designation;
- mapping scope;
- effective period;
- synchronization policy;
- recorded time.

An external identifier never replaces the local principal identity.

### 13. Database and transport keys are not domain identity

Auto-increment keys, document-store keys, graph-store keys, message offsets, filenames, URLs, and repository commits may be implementation locators or indexes. They shall not become the sole durable identity exposed across context boundaries.

### 14. Governed history does not permit identity reassignment

Withdrawal, redaction, anonymization, deletion, cryptographic erasure, or retention expiry may remove content, mappings, or identifying residue according to policy.

No governed-history action permits reassignment of an identifier to unrelated information.

Where policy requires no retained identifier, the identifier may become unresolvable rather than reusable.

### 15. Generation and collision handling

Identifiers may be generated by authorized clients, services, import processes, or offline tools.

Every accepting boundary shall validate syntax and reject an identifier collision that refers to different domain identity.

Collision response is a recorded failure; automatic reassignment is permitted only before the identifier has been acknowledged externally or used in a consequential record.

## Consequences

### Positive

- Identity survives repository and technology replacement.
- Bounded contexts can generate identifiers independently.
- Cross-context references do not depend on internal database keys.
- Backlinks, Trails, manifests, exports, and audit records share one durable addressing convention.
- Content hashes can evolve independently from domain identity.
- Governed erasure does not create identifier reuse risk.

### Costs

- Every context must preserve identity separately from display names and storage locations.
- Region identity requires editor and serialization support.
- Importers must maintain explicit external mappings.
- Implementations must detect collisions and prevent accidental identity replacement.
- Stable Trail Step identity requires deliberate revision semantics.

### Risks

- Teams may incorrectly infer chronology from UUID ordering.
- Implementations may expose raw identifiers without human-readable context.
- Region or Step identity may be preserved too aggressively across conceptual replacement.
- Content digests may be mistaken for domain identity.

These risks are addressed through validation, human-readable presentation, and later ADRs for serialization, anchoring, and lifecycle.

## Rejected Alternatives

### Repository path as identity

Rejected because paths are mutable and implementation-specific.

### Database-generated integer as cross-context identity

Rejected because integers are not globally unique and couple consumers to one datastore.

### Content hash as principal identity

Rejected because mutable Resources require stable identity across changing content and canonicalization rules.

### External-system identifier as principal identity

Rejected because authority, provider, and mapping may change and one Resource may have several external identities.

### Type-prefixed semantic identifiers

Rejected as principal identity because type and ownership can evolve. Human-readable aliases may exist but remain separate from durable identity.

## Verification Expectations

Implementation verification shall eventually demonstrate:

1. identity survives renaming and movement;
2. Revision identity remains distinct from Resource identity;
3. identifiers are not reused after deletion or withdrawal;
4. imported external identifiers map without replacing local identity;
5. backlinks resolve through Relationship Identity;
6. Region identity survives ordinary movement without mis-targeting;
7. exports and independent reconstruction preserve identities;
8. collision attempts fail safely;
9. Projection provenance identifies exact definition and inputs.
